Advincula

Key Point
  • This article provides a step by step description of the surgical approach and practical tips for the total laparoscopic hysterectomy (TLH) procedure.
  • Surgeon uses the PKS Cutting Forceps for hemostasis during the procedure and a monopolor device for the colpotomy.
  • The author mentions an advanced bipolar device can be used on the colpotomy but does not identify the device.
Reference
  • Advincula, A. Total Laparoscopic Hysterectomy: Practical Tips and Strategies. Female Patient.

AAGL

Key Point
  • This special article represents the AAGL's (American Association of Gynecologic Laparoscopists) position regarding the laparoscopic and vaginal vs. open approach for hysterectomy.
  • "It is the position of the AAGL that most hysterectomies for benign disease should be performed either vaginally or laparoscopically and that continued efforts should be taken to facilitate these approaches."
  • "When hysterectomy is necessary, the demonstrated safety, efficacy, and cost-effectiveness of vaginal hysterectomy and laparoscopic hysterectomy mandate that they be the procedures of choice."
  • "When hysterectomy is performed without laparotomy, early institutional discharge is feasible and safe, in many cases within the first 24 hours."
  • Laparoscopy reduced the indirect costs of hysterectomy by as much as 50% when compared to abdominal hysterectomy.
  • "A number of clinical situations considered as contraindications to laparoscopic hysterectomy seem not to have merit when subjected to critical analysis". These include obesity and previous caesarian section.
  • "If a large uterus or other coexisting disease or surgical considerations render vaginal hysterectomy not feasible, laparoscopic hysterectomy seems to be a safe alternative that preserves most of the advantages of vaginal hysterectomy over abdominal hysterectomy."
Reference
  • AAGL, AAGL Position Statement: Route of Hysterectomy to Treat Benign Uterine Disease. AAGL Advancing Minimally Invasive Gynecology Worldwide. J. Minimally Invasive Gynecol. 2010 Nov. 3.

ACOG

Key Point
  • This special article represents the ACOG's (American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists) position regarding the supracervical vs. total hysterectomy.
  • "The supracervical approach should not be recommended by the surgeon as a superior technique for hysterectomy for benign disease."
  • "Choosing to preserve the cervix to reduce adverse effects of hysterectomy on sexual and urinary function is not supported by data from prospective randomized trials."
  • "Despite the potential advantages of shorter hospital stay and less blood loss afforded by the laparoscopic supracervical approach, there are no prospective data comparing laparoscopic supracervical hysterectomy with either LAVH or TAH."
  • "Women with known or suspected gynecologic cancer, current or recent cervical dysplasia, or endometrial hypstplasia are not candidates for a supracervical procedure."
Reference
  • ACOG, AACOG Committee Opinion No. 388 November 2007: Supracervical Hysterectomy. American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists. Obstet Gynecol. 2007 Nov 110 (5): 1215-7.

Barbash

Key Point
  • Examined the variable cost of conducting a robot assisted procedure across 20 types of surgery, including gynecology.
    - Mean cost per procedure for robotic laparoscopic hysterectomy: $8,951.
    - No data on what these costs include.
    - For more details on cost of the robot for gynecological procedures, please reference the following authors: Advincula et al. (2007) and Pasic et al. (2010).
  • Learning curve of 150-250 procedures depending on procedure and surgeon skill level.
    - For more information regarding robot learning curves please reference: Carreyrou (2010), Lenihan et al. (2008), Payne et al. (2008), and Steinberg et al. (2008).
  • The additional cost for the robot assisted procedure is 6% (13% when including the amortized cost of the robot itself) of the cost of the procedure in 2007.
  • Relating to prostatectomy procedure: "...evidence suggests that despite the short-term benefits, robotic technology may not have improved patient outcomes or quality of life in the long run".
  • "Number of robot assisted procedures that are performed worldwide has nearly tripled since 2007, from 80,000 to 205,000."
  • Between 2007 and 2009, the number of da Vinci systems that were installed in U.S. hospitals grew by approximately 75%, from almost 800 to around 1400, while the number abroad doubled from 200 to nearly 400.
Reference
  • Barbash G., Glied S. New Technology and Health Care Costs - The Case of Robot-Assisted Surgery. The New England Journal of Medicine. 2010; 363 (8); p. 701-704.

Brill

Key Point
  • This article is written as a transcript of a conversation among a few prominent gynecological surgeons.
  • They discuss the advantages of advanced radio frequency energy in gynecological surgery.
  • "The Gyrus [instrumentation] is the closest cousin to the Kleppinger and therefore the most intuitive of these new devices for the gynecologic surgeon to use."
  • Dr. Bertrand suggests that the Gyrus PKS Cutting Forceps are, "suitable for surgeons who wish to move on to more advanced technology and have experience using the Kleppinger device."
  • All surgeons quoted in article have disclosures with Ethicon.
Reference
  • Brill A., Bertrand J., McCarus S. Advanced Energy Systems For Laparoscopic Gynecology Procedures. OBG Management. November 2009; 21 (11).

Carreyrou

Key Point
  • This interesting article explores the success of Intuitive Surgical and the risks involved with the learning curve of the robot at one small hospital in New Hampshire, Wentworth-Douglass.
  • The article discusses the challenges and complication rates for a hospital that has a low number of cases per year.
  • When a new hospital purchases a robot, Intuitive Surgical provides a paid two-day course for two surgeons at a training center.
  • The article reports that Jim Hu, a surgeon at Brigham and Women's Hospital in Boston, who has done more than 1,000 surgeries with the robot, says it takes a urologist anywhere from 250 to 700 cases to master it.
Reference
  • Carreyrou, J. Surgical Robot Examined in Injuries. Wall Street Journal Online. 5/4/2010.

Levy

Key Point
  • Article is an overview of vessel-sealing devices for laparoscopic procedures including: PKS Cutting Forceps and Plasma Trissector (Trissector is no longer sold), Harmonic Scalpel, Ligamax 5 Endoscopic Multiple Clip Applier, EnSeal Tissue Sealing and Hemostasis System and EnSeal PTC, and LigaSure V.
  • Dr. Levy's conclusions seem to be based on opinion rather than factual clinical data.
    - No numerical data were presented related to burst pressure.
    - Please reference Lim et al. (1999) for systolic blood pressure and the "Securing Vessels" tab.
  • Article does not favorably portray the Gyrus PKS system.
  • Key Points relating to "Securing Vessels" for the PK System:
    - PK devices, specifically the PKS Cutting Forceps, have been providing adequate hemostasis for over 10 years without a need for a vessel-sealing claim.
    - The PK platform has the largest array of instrument tips for the surgeon's specific needs.
    - There is a 7 mm vessel-sealing claim for the PKS SEAL Open forceps.
    - The PKS Cutting Forceps has serrated jaws to grasp onto tissue whereas tissue tends to slip through the jaws with LigaSure.
    - PK is the only platform that provides surgeons with true bipolar cut and bipolar coagulation in one instrument. LigaSure can coagulate with bipolar but has a monopolar cut with the Advance device.
  • The article references Newcomb et al. (2009), and Lamberton et al. (2008).
Reference
  • Levy, B. Vessel-Sealing Devices. OBG Management. September 2009; 21 (9).

Mohr

Key Point
  • Article by the Director of Medical Research at Intuitive Surgical about why robotic surgery saves hospitals money.
  • Average per procedure costs for robotic is $4,000 (Costs include instruments, amoritization and maintenance) as compared to a $900 cost per procedure for laparoscopic procedure. That is a $3,000 difference per procedure.
  • Article does not specify which laparoscopic procedure.
  • Article discusses cost savings with robotic procedures related to shorter length of stay (LOS) and reduced complications.
  • LOS cost savings is compared to open and the author conveniently ignores laparoscopy. Reduced complications savings not specific to specialty and can be misleading because all specialties including urology are included.
  • For more information regarding the robot, please see the "da Vinci" tab.
Reference
  • Mohr, C. Is Robotic Surgery Cheaper? New York Times Online. 7/20/2010.

Quintero

Key Point
  • Article provides a counter viewpoint compared to the Wall Street Journal (written by Carreyrou) on Wentworth-Douglass Hospital.
  • Examines the different training programs implemented by hospitals with robotics programs.
  • Surgeons with extensive robot experience say it takes at least 200 surgeries to become proficient with the robot.
  • Dr. Vip Patel, Urologist in Celebration Florida, states, "Surgeons need at least 50-100 cases to become proficient with the robot."
  • Opinions on the learning curve for robotic surgery vary and depend on the skill of the surgeon, previous experience in laparoscopic surgery, number of cases performed on a robot, and number of cases the hospital (surgeon) performs to increase experience level.
Reference
  • Quintero, F. Robotic Surgery: How Safe Is It? Orlando Sentinel Online. 7/19/2010.
